What I do !!

➤ Research Focus: Unveiling the Internal Structure of Hadrons

My primary goal is to understand the internal structure of hadrons and explore their intrinsic properties through modern theoretical frameworks, especially within light-front dynamics.

⟶ Transverse Momentum Dependent Distributions (TMDs):

I have studied model-dependent TMDs for:

  • Spin-0: Pion, Kaon
  • Spin-1/2: Proton
  • Spin-1: Vector Mesons, Photon
Along with TMDs, I have calculated Parton Distribution Functions (PDFs) and spin densities in momentum space, providing a 3D understanding of partonic structures. These are vital for the physics programs at the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC).I have calculated the higher twist for WW aproximations, however the higher Fock-state calculations going on to calculate the pure twist effects.

⟶ Generalized Parton Distributions (GPDs) and GDAs:

I have calculated GPDs for:

  • Spin-0: Pion and kaon
  • Spin-1: Rho meson
From these, I extract electromagnetic and gravitational form factors. I have also initiated the computation of Generalized Distribution Amplitudes (GDAs) for the pion, relevant to exclusive processes at JLab and EIC. We have also calculate the Cross section for rho meson. I am currently calculating the mechanical properties of spin-1 mesons.

⟶ Hadronic Properties:

Using light-front quark models, I have worked on:

  • Spin-orbit correlations in mesons
  • Distribution amplitudes (DAs)
  • Form factors, charge radii, decay constants
  • Mass spectra and radially excited states of quarkonia
  • Radiative transitions of charmonia
  • Mechanical properties

⟶ Medium Modifications:

In collaboration, I am studying the modification of distribution functions (TMDs, PDFs, GPDs) in a nuclear or hadronic medium using:

  • Light-Front Quark Model (LFQM)
  • Light-Front Holographic QCD (LFHQCD)
  • Chiral SU(3) Quark Mean Field Model
These are crucial to understanding quark behavior in nuclear environments such as compact stars and heavy-ion collisions.

My research strongly aligns with the goals of next-generation experimental programs such as the Electron-Ion Collider (EIC) and Jefferson Lab (JLab).

Why I Do It!!

I’m endlessly curious about how matter is built — and how the invisible structure beneath hadrons shapes the world around us.

What began as a fascination with quantum mechanics and beautiful equations has grown into a deeper mission: to explore the inner landscapes of protons, neutrons, and mesons. Through this, I seek to uncover how the fundamental forces govern the visible universe.

To me, fundamental physics doesn’t just answer “how” — it dares to ask “why.”
Why do particles have mass?
Why does confinement exist?
Why does the universe behave the way it does?

Each layer I peel away from hadronic structure takes me a step closer to these truths. And more than anything — I do it for the thrill of chasing the unknown, solving puzzles that no one has cracked yet.

How I Do It!!

I combine analytical techniques with computational simulations, working extensively with:

  • Light-front quantization
  • Effective models such as the Nambu–Jona-Lasinio (NJL) model
  • Light-Front Quark Models (LFQM)

Using these frameworks, I derive theoretical expressions for observables like form factors, pressure distributions, and spin-orbit correlations. These are then simulated using symbolic programming in Mathematica or numerical routines in Python.

My approach is both collaborative and data-informed. I frequently compare model predictions with experimental data and lattice QCD results, adjusting model parameters and extending frameworks as necessary.

Whether it's constructing helicity amplitude matrices for spin-1 hadrons, or extracting Transverse Momentum Dependent Distributions (TMDs) from correlators, my work remains rigorous, step-by-step, and physically grounded.

Master Project

Master Thesis !!

➤ Title:

On the Proton-Oxygen Elastic Scattering by Taylor Series Expansion

➤ Abstract:

A simple method using the Taylor series expansion of the wave function is employed to construct a phase-equivalent energy-dependent local potential corresponding to a combination of local and nonlocal interactions, expanded up to order n = 3. The resulting potentials are used to calculate scattering phase shifts via the phase function method. The approach is applied to the proton–oxygen system, and the results show reasonable agreement with standard experimental data, validating the effectiveness of this analytical technique for nuclear scattering problems.

B. Sc Project !!

➤ Title:

Superconductivity and Its Application to Maglev Trains

➤ Abstract:

This project explores the fundamental principles of superconductivity—a quantum phenomenon where certain materials exhibit zero electrical resistance and the expulsion of magnetic fields below a critical temperature. Emphasis is placed on the Meissner effect and type-II superconductors, which enable magnetic levitation. The project highlights how these principles are practically applied in Maglev (magnetic levitation) train technology, allowing high-speed, frictionless transportation. Theoretical insights are complemented with real-world engineering examples to show how superconductors revolutionize transport through minimal energy loss and superior efficiency.
